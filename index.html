What he inherited — Paternal
A grandfather, Shimon, who came to Prešov on an uncle's invitation, fell in love with his cousin, built a liqueur empire, consulted the Munkacser Rebbe before buying it, sent a daughter to run the factory at fourteen during WWI, and gave a Torah scroll to the shul in grief for a daughter lost. Through his mother Teresa Bernfeld: a documented rabbinic chain from the Megale Amukot through the Löw-Beer dynasty of Boskovice — the same family whose descendants built Villa Tugendhat in Brno, today a UNESCO World Heritage Site. His name

The smell of plum liqueur and raspberry syrup from the factory courtyard behind the apartment. A spacious home in a city where the Mullers were known.

Chapter I — The Ancient Chain
From Troyes to Kraków: The Rabbinic Lineage Oral tradition

The chain this family claims begins in Troyes, Champagne, around 1040 CE — with Rabbi Shlomo Yitzchaki, known as Rashi. The foremost commentator on Torah and Talmud, whose words still sit on every page of standard editions. His lineage passed through daughters and descendants eastward across the Rhineland, into Poland, and into the Shapira dynasty.

By 1585, that chain reaches Rabbi Natan Neta Shapira in Kraków — the Megale Amukot, one of the greatest kabbalistic works of the 17th century. His descent from Rashi is documented. From the Shapira line, through subsequent generations, the rabbinic heritage enters the Bernfeld family — and through Teresa Bernfeld, Robert's mother, it reaches Prešov and Robert himself.

Note on sourcing: The direct patrilineal link between Rabbi Samuel Kolin and the Shapira/Spira family is not confirmed by academic sources. The connection more likely runs through Samuel's wife's line. The Rashi → Shapira chain itself is well-established; the link to the Kolin family carries the nature of oral genealogical tradition. This archive presents it as such.

Sources: Encyclopaedia Judaica (Shapira entry); Geni.com lineage trees; research notes (Denise Karasik)

Chapter II — The Patriarch
Ahron Muller & Pesel Lieberman — Teachers from Sanz

Ahron Muller, born around 1852, was a teacher — not a rabbi, but a man shaped by the serious intellectual life of Modern Orthodox Judaism. He studied at Rabbi Esriel Hildesheimer's Rabbinical Seminary in Berlin, the flagship institution that combined rigorous Torah scholarship with secular academic standards. This was a deliberate, considered choice: Hildesheimer's seminary represented a particular way of being Jewish in the modern world — serious, educated, engaged.

He married Pesel Lieberman. Together they raised their son Shimon in Nowy Sącz — known in Jewish tradition as Sanz, home of the great Halberstam Hasidic dynasty. The world Shimon grew up in was shaped by both his father's Modern Orthodox seriousness and the intense Hasidic culture of the city around him.

Chapter III — Arrival in Prešov
The Invitation: From Sanz to Prešov

An invitation changed the course of the family's history. Moshe Aryeh Rosman — Shimon's uncle, his father's brother — called him to Prešov to work in his dry goods store. Come learn the trade, earn a livelihood. Shimon came.

While working in his uncle's store, he met Sara — Moshe Aryeh's own daughter. His cousin. He fell in love with her. Sara had a prior betrothal, a long-standing commitment to another family. She broke it to marry Shimon. The family was in an uproar. But the marriage held.

Shimon and Sara settled in Prešov and raised six children: Ilona (Denise's Oma), Rosie (Raizel), Yakov, Margit (Malca), Pirike (Perel), and Josi. Prešov — a royal free town in Upper Hungary, today northeast Slovakia — became their permanent home. Three generations of Mullers would live here.

The Prešov Orthodox Cemetery records independently confirm this: the Hebrew epitaphs record Moshe Ariel Rozman, his wife Rivka, and their daughter Sara wife of Simon Miller/Muller — a perfect match for the family account, confirmed in stone.

Sources: Oma's Story (Denise Karasik) · Prešov Orthodox Cemetery records (JewishGen / Futtersack)

Chapter IV — The Businessman
Cheese, Plum Liqueur, and the Munkacser Rebbe's Blessing

After years working for his father-in-law, Shimon's first independent venture was a cheese factory, co-owned with partners Grosswirt and Schnitzer. Business was in his nature.

Then came the opportunity to acquire Schwartz & Glassner — a fruit liqueur enterprise. Before committing, Shimon did what a serious man of his world did: he consulted the Munkacser Rebbe, one of the most revered Hasidic authorities of the era. The Rebbe advised him to buy. He did.

The business flourished. The factory produced plum liqueurs and raspberry syrups, stored in great multi-colored glass vats, and shipped as far as Germany. The factory courtyard sat directly behind the family's spacious apartment — the smell of fruit always nearby. For the next generation of Mullers, this factory was the sound and smell of home.

Source: Oma's Story — Denise Karasik's grandmother Ilona's account

Chapter V — World War I
A Fourteen-Year-Old Runs the Factory

During World War I, the army requisitioned Shimon's forest for timber. Russian prisoners of war were sent to log it; Shimon had to travel there to supervise, away from Prešov for long stretches.

The factory could not stop. So Shimon taught his daughter Ilona — Denise's Oma, fourteen years old — how to run it entirely: balancing the ledgers, managing the workers, operating the bottling machinery, which was powerful and potentially dangerous. She did it. The daughter he trusted most with the work he had built.

Denise's Oma's written account of this, preserved decades later, carries no complaint — only the quiet pride of someone who was given something serious to do, and did it.

Source: Oma's Story — Denise Karasik's grandmother Ilona's firsthand account

Chapter VI — Grief & Faith
Margit's Death — A Torah Scroll for the Shul

Tragedy came through his daughter Margit (Malca), who died from internal injuries after jumping from the wrong train. The loss cut deeply into the family.

Shimon's response was neither silence nor collapse. He commissioned a Sefer Torah to be written and donated it to the Prešov shul in Margit's memory. A man expressing grief the only way that seemed adequate: by giving something eternal to the community she had been part of.

A cemetery record from Prešov — "Panna Malke bas Simon", buried September 1923 — lists a daughter of Simon who predeceased her mother Sara, likely Margit herself. The stone and the Torah scroll, taken together: a father keeping his daughter present.

Sources: Oma's Story (Denise Karasik) · Prešov Orthodox Cemetery records (JewishGen)

Chapter VII — Father & Grandfather
Frankfurt, Remarriage, and Sunday Excursions

When it came time for his son Yakov to attend Yeshiva, the Frankfurt institution was hesitant — reluctant to accept a boy from a strict local Yeshiva in Prešov. Shimon traveled to Frankfurt personally and advocated for ten days until they relented. A father who knew that access to the right education was worth a journey and a fight.

After Sara's death from an infected cut, Shimon remarried Malvinka Fonfedder — Sara's cousin, who had grown up attending the same school as Sara. A remarriage within the circle of memory.

As a grandfather, he was devoted. He loved taking his grandchildren on Sunday excursions outside the city — by truck or horse and wagon. Robert was born in 1931 while Shimon was very much the living center of the family. In those early years, the factory courtyard, the Sunday rides, the grandfather who had fought ten days in Frankfurt for Yakov's education — this was the world Robert came into.

Source: Oma's Story — Denise Karasik's grandmother Ilona's account

Chapter VIII — The Shoah
Budapest, 1944 — The Last Blessing

As German occupation advanced through Hungary in 1944, the family's options collapsed. Shimon first stayed with his daughter Rosi in Munkacs — but he spoke no local language and was too visibly a foreigner, too easily identified. He was smuggled to Miskolc to live with Denise's Oma, Ilona.

The family made a plan to flee to Budapest with forged Aryan papers. They boarded the train. They arrived at Budapest station. Shimon was identified as Jewish, arrested, and taken to prison.

His granddaughter Gretl — herself captured and held in the same prison — spotted him across the yard during a registration lineup. She ran to him. He placed his hands on her head. He gave her his blessing.

A guard chased her away.

That was the last time anyone in the family saw Shimon Muller. He was deported from Budapest to Auschwitz, where he was murdered by the Nazis.

Sources: Oma's Story (Denise Karasik) · Hungarian Jewish deportation records, 1944 · Yad Vashem

Chapter IX — The Destination
Robert Moyshe Arie Muller — 1931, Prešov

Born 1931 in Prešov. Firstborn of Yakov Muller and Teresa Bernfeld. He came into a family that was still whole — grandfather Shimon alive and taking grandchildren on Sunday truck rides, the factory humming in the courtyard, the community still intact.

His Hebrew name Moyshe Arie echoes Moshe Aryeh Rosman — the great-uncle whose invitation to Prešov had started everything. Through the name, that founding act is remembered in every generation that follows.

Through his mother Teresa Bernfeld, Robert carries the documented rabbinic chain: Löw-Beer of Boskovice → Rabbi Samuel Kolin → Shapira of Kraków → and, by family tradition, back to Rashi of Troyes. Through his father Yakov, he carries the Sanz-born Muller line, the Hildesheimer scholar, the liqueur factory, the Munkacser Rebbe's blessing.

A thousand years of migration, scholarship, grief, and survival — arriving here, in this person, in Prešov, in 1931.

Sources: Oma's Story (Denise Karasik), Prešov cemetery records, Topolčany Yizkor Book, Geni, Ancestry, Encyclopaedia Judaica

The Family Names

Each surname a world — a community, a history, a migration

Muller
מִילֶר
Primary Line

German occupational name meaning "miller," widely adopted by Ashkenazi Jews during the Habsburg surname decree (~1787–1804). Four confirmed generations: Ahron → Shimon → Yakov → Robert. Transliterated in Prešov cemetery records as "Miller" — Hebrew makes no distinction.

Nowy Sącz → Prešov · confirmed · cemetery records independent verification

Rosman
רוסמן
The Prešov Anchor

Moshe Aryeh Rosman invited Shimon to Prešov and gave the Muller family its foothold in Slovakia. His daughter Sara became Shimon's wife — making Rosman the maternal root of Yakov. Robert's Hebrew name Moyshe Arie directly echoes Moshe Aryeh Rosman. Confirmed independently by Prešov Orthodox Cemetery epitaphs.

Shimon's wife Sara · Source: Oma's Story (Denise Karasik) + cemetery records

Bernfeld
ברנפלד
Lineage Carrier — Löw-Beer & Shapira Chain

Moravian/Galician family confirmed as prominent leaders of the Topolčany Jewish community. They connect through marriage to the Löw-Beer industrial dynasty. Through Teresa Bernfeld, Robert's mother, this entire chain enters the Muller family. The Bernfeld family also originated in Boskovice, Moravia — same town as the Löw-Beers.

Teresa Bernfeld = Robert's mother · Topolčany Yizkor Book confirmed

Löw-Beer
לוֹאו-בֵּר
Moravian Dynasty — via Bernfeld

Prominent Moravian Jewish industrialists whose descendants built Villa Tugendhat in Brno — a UNESCO World Heritage Site designed by Mies van der Rohe. The name fuses Löw (from Rabbi Kolin's Löw family) and Beer (from Buna Beer, Jehuda's wife) — created after the 1788 Josefinian surname decree. Connection to the Muller family runs through the Bernfeld–Löw-Beer marriage.

Via Bernfeld–Löw-Beer marriage · Czech Wikipedia, Encyclopaedia Judaica confirmed

Kolin
קולין
Rabbinic Root — Boskovice

Rabbi Samuel Ha-Levi Kolin (c. 1720–1806), born in Kolín, Bohemia, presided over the Boskovice yeshiva for nearly 60 years. His son Jehuda Löw married Buna Beer; their son Salomon took the name Löw-Beer. The Kolin connection is paternal — through Jehuda Löw, not through Perl/Paulina (Salomon's wife).

Patrilineal: Kolin → Jehuda Löw → Salomon Löw-Beer · Encyclopaedia Judaica confirmed

Shapira
שפירא
Rabbinic Ancestry — via Bernfeld/Kolin

From Speyer, Germany. One of the great Ashkenazi rabbinic dynasties. The Kraków branch produced the Megale Amukot. The connection to the Kolin/Löw-Beer line likely runs through Rabbi Samuel Kolin's wife's family — academically unverified as a direct patrilineal link, but carried as family tradition.

Tradition: Shapira → Kolin → Löw-Beer → Bernfeld · oral tradition, flag accordingly
