Medieval Period · ~1040–1400
~1040 CE · Troyes, Champagne
Rabbi Shlomo Yitzchaki — Rashi רש״י
רַשִׁ״י — רַבֵּנוּ שְׁלֹמֹה יִצְחָקִי
Troyes, France → Worms, Germany

The foremost commentator on Torah and Talmud, born ~1040 in Troyes, died 1105. His commentaries appear in all standard editions of both. He had no male heirs — the lineage passes through daughters into the Rhineland Tosafist tradition, eventually reaching the Spira/Shapira dynasty of Poland. The descent Rashi → Treves → Spira → Luria → Shapira → Kolin is documented in traditional rabbinic genealogical literature (yuchasin), though not confirmed by peer-reviewed academic sources. Present as oral tradition.

Oral Tradition Chain Anchor
~1300s–1573 · Rhineland → Poland
Treves · Spira · Luria — The Eastward Migration
Paris → Speyer → Lublin

Mattitjahu Treves, Chief Rabbi of Paris (~1300s), begets the Spira family — their name from Speyer (Latin: Spira). Rabbi Shlomo Spira (~1375–1452) receives a yeshiva permit in 1397. His daughter Miriam Spira marries Aaron I Luria of Heilbronn (~1413), merging the two great lineages. Their descendant Rabbi Solomon Luria (MaHaRaSHaL, 1510–1573) heads the Lublin Yeshiva — and the line moves further east into Kraków.

Early Modern · 1585–1724
1585–1633 · Kraków
Rabbi Nathan Neta Shapira — Megale Amukot
ר' נָתָן נְטַע שַׁפִּירָא — מְגַלֶּה עֲמוּקוֹת
Kraków, Poland — Chief Rabbi

One of the most celebrated kabbalists of the 17th century, author of the Megale Amukot ("Revealer of Depths"), and Chief Rabbi of Kraków. His Geni profile and Wikipedia entry confirm biographical details. The Bernfeld family — who enter this story through Robert's mother Tereza-Devora — carry lineage through the Shapira dynasty.

The connection between Shapira and Rabbi Samuel Kolin flows through Kolin's wife (identified in genealogical sources as "a daughter Spira"), not through his patrilineal descent. The name "Nathan Neta ha-Levi" echoed in the Kolin family may have caused genealogical conflation. Present as documented tradition, not established academic fact.

Boskovice — Kolin — Löw-Beer · 1724–1870
~1724–1806 · Boskovice, Moravia
Rabbi Samuel ben Nathan Ha-Levi Kolin — Machatzit HaShekel
ר' שְׁמוּאֵל בֶּן נָתָן הַלֵּוִי קוֹלִין
Boskovice, Moravia — Head of Yeshiva ~60 years

Born ~1724 in Kolín, Bohemia; died 20 May 1806 in Boskovice. Presided over the Boskovice yeshiva for nearly 60 years — one of the longest tenures of any Moravian rabbinical figure. Authored the Machatzit HaShekel, a sub-commentary on the Shulchan Aruch that appears in all standard editions to this day.

Key correction: The Kolin → Löw-Beer link is PATERNAL. Rabbi Samuel → his son Jehuda Löw (who married Buna Beer) → their son Salomon Löw-Beer. The surname fuses both parents' family identities after the 1788 Josefinian decree. Perl/Paulina was Salomon's wife, not the Kolin connection.

Salomon Löw-Beer — First of the Dynasty Name
שְׁלֹמֹה לֶב-בֵּיר

Son of Jehuda Löw; first to carry the compound Löw-Beer surname. Served as judge and mayor of the Boskovice ghetto. His two sons launched two distinct dynasties: Moses (→ Svitávka textile empire, Villa Tugendhat) and Abraham (→ Slovakia — our branch). He married Paulina Perl (née Pavlina).

1839–1930s · Svitávka → Brno
Moses Löw-Beer — The Industrial Dynasty (parallel branch)
מֹשֶׁה לֶב-בֵּיר
Svitávka → Brno — Villa Tugendhat

Moses Löw-Beer (c. 1794–1851), Salomon's son, founded a wool-spinning factory in Svitávka around 1839–1843. His son Maximilian "Max" Löw-Beer (1829–1887) transformed it into Austria's most important producer of cheap textile goods. A second branch — Aron & Jakob Löw-Beer — established a factory in Brněnec which in 1944 became Schindler's factory, saving nearly 1,200 Jews.

Alfred Löw-Beer (1872–1939) financed Villa Tugendhat — designed by Ludwig Mies van der Rohe in 1929–30 — as a wedding gift to his daughter Grete. It became a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2001. This is the parallel line; our branch descends through Abraham, not Moses.

1840–1926 · Topolčany
Moshe Bernfeld + Gittel Löw-Beer — The Topolčany Nexus
מֹשֶׁה בֶּרְנְפֶלְד + גִּיטֶל לֶב-בֵּיר
Topolčany — Deputy Chairman, Community Repräsentanz

Moshe (Mor) Bernfeld (1840–1926), born and died in Topolčany, married Gittel (Katalin) née Löw-Beer — directly connecting the Bernfeld family to the Löw-Beer dynasty. His brother Jónás Bernfeld was born in Boskovice in 1831, confirming that the Bernfeld family shared Moravian roots with the Löw-Beers. This is the confirmed linchpin of the two dynasties' union.

The Topolčany Yizkor Book names Moshe as deputy chairman of the pre-WWI Repräsentanz. The Bernfeld family built a Beth Medrash at the market square. Moshe and Gittel had at least 17 children. Among them: Max (Mordechai) Bernfeld, Gabbai of the Great Synagogue — whose daughter Tereza-Devora would marry Yaakov Dov Muller of Prešov.

Topolčany → survived Holocaust → Haifa, Israel

Max (Mordechai) Bernfeld (1884–1964), son of Moshe + Gittel, served as Gabbai of the Great Synagogue of Topolčany. He married Erna Ester Herzfeld (born 20 July 1888 in Brezova; died 1 February 1966 in Haifa). Note: the Herzfeld family was from Brezova, not Topolčany — entirely absent from the Yizkor Book. Erna's parents were Meir Herzfeld (1847–1916) and Tereza Dvora née Löw-Beer (daughter of Nathan Nota).

Max and Erna survived the Holocaust and emigrated to Israel. Their brother Sámuel Yehuda Bernfeld (1876–c. 1944) was murdered at Auschwitz. Among their five children: Tereza-Devora Bernfeld, born 12 December 1909 — who would become Robert Muller's mother.

Shimon Muller

שִׁמְעוֹן מִילֶר · b. ~1874–1889, Nowy Sącz · d. 1944, Auschwitz · Based on "Oma's Story" — Ilona's account, transcribed by family

Chapter I — Origins
From Sanz to Prešov: The Invitation

Shimon Muller was born in Nowy Sącz — known in Jewish tradition as Sanz — the Galician town home to the great Halberstam Hasidic dynasty. He was the son of Ahron Muller, a teacher trained at the Hildesheimer Rabbinical Seminary in Berlin, and Pesel Lieberman.

An invitation changed the course of his life. His uncle Moshe Aryeh Rozman called him to Prešov to work in his dry goods business — to learn the trade and earn a livelihood. Shimon came. He learned. And while working in his uncle's store, he met Sara — Moshe Aryeh's own daughter, his cousin.

Sara broke a long-standing betrothal to marry him. The family was in an uproar. But the marriage held. Shimon and Sara settled in Prešov and raised six children: Ilona (Oma), Rosie (Raizel), Yakov, Margit (Malca), Pirike (Perel), and Josi.

Chapter II — The Businessman
Cheese, Plum Liqueur, and the Munkacser Rebbe's Blessing

After years working for his father-in-law, Shimon's first independent venture was a cheese factory, co-owned with partners Grosswirt and Schnitzer. Business was in his nature.

Then came the opportunity to acquire Schwartz & Glassner — a fruit liqueur enterprise. Before committing, Shimon consulted the Munkacser Rebbe, one of the most revered Hasidic authorities of the era. The Rebbe advised him to buy. He did. The business flourished.

The factory produced plum liqueurs and raspberry syrups, stored in great multi-colored glass vats, and shipped as far as Germany. The factory courtyard was directly behind the family's spacious apartment — the smell of fruit always close.

Chapter III — War
A Fourteen-Year-Old Runs the Factory

During World War I, the army requisitioned Shimon's forest for timber. Russian prisoners of war were sent to log it; Shimon had to travel there to supervise, away from Prešov for long stretches.

The factory could not stop. So Shimon taught his daughter Ilona — fourteen years old — how to run it entirely: balancing the ledgers, managing the workers, operating the bottling machinery, which was powerful and potentially dangerous. She did it. The daughter he trusted most with the work he had built.

Ilona's account of this, written decades later, carries no complaint — only the quiet pride of someone who was given something serious to do, and did it.

Chapter IV — Grief and Faith
Margit's Death · A Torah Scroll for the Shul

Tragedy came through his daughter Margit (Malca), who died from internal injuries after jumping from the wrong train. The loss cut deeply.

Shimon's response was neither silence nor collapse. He commissioned a Sefer Torah to be written and donated it to the Prešov shul in Margit's memory. A man expressing grief the only way that seemed adequate: by giving something eternal to the community she had been part of.

The Prešov Orthodox Cemetery records confirm a "Panna Malke bas Simon" buried in September 1923 — almost certainly Margit, providing independent corroboration of Ilona's account.

Chapter V — Father and Grandfather
Frankfurt, Second Marriage, and Sunday Excursions

When it came time for his son Yakov to attend Yeshiva, the Frankfurt institution was hesitant — reluctant to take a boy from a strict local Yeshiva in Prešov. Shimon traveled to Frankfurt personally and advocated for ten days until they relented.

After Sara's death from an infected cut, Shimon remarried Malvinka Fonfedder — Sara's cousin, who had grown up attending the same school as Sara. A remarriage within the circle of memory.

Yakov would go on to marry Tereza-Devora Bernfeld of Topolčany, daughter of Max (Mordechai) Bernfeld and Erna Herzfeld — connecting the Muller family of Prešov to the Bernfeld–Löw-Beer dynasty of western Slovakia and ultimately to the Kolin–Boskovice rabbinical chain. Their son Robert was born in 1931.

As a grandfather, Shimon was devoted. He loved taking his grandchildren on Sunday excursions outside the city — by truck or horse and wagon. Robert was born while Shimon was very much the living center of the family.

Chapter VI — The Shoah
Budapest, 1944 — The Last Blessing

As German occupation advanced through Hungary in 1944, the family's options collapsed. Shimon first stayed with his daughter Rosi in Munkacs — but he spoke no local language and was too visibly a foreigner. He was smuggled to Miskolc to live with Ilona.

The family made a plan to flee to Budapest with forged Aryan papers. They boarded the train. They arrived at Budapest station. Shimon was identified as Jewish, arrested, and taken to prison.

His granddaughter Gretl — herself captured and held in the same prison — spotted him across the yard during a registration lineup. She ran to him. He placed his hands on her head. He gave her his blessing.

A guard chased her away.

That was the last time anyone in the family saw Shimon Muller. He was deported from Budapest to Auschwitz, where he was murdered by the Nazis in 1944.

Löw-Beer
לוֹאו-בֵּר
Boskovice Dynasty — via Bernfeld

A compound surname fusing two family identities after the 1788 Josefinian decree: Löw (lion) from the Kolin/HaLevi rabbinical line, Beer (bear) from wife Buna's family. Four generations from Boskovice to Topolčany: Jehuda Löw → Salomon Löw-Beer → Abraham → Nathan Nota. The Svitávka branch built Villa Tugendhat (UNESCO). The Brněnec branch became Schindler's factory. Our branch → Topolčany → Bernfeld → Tereza-Devora → Robert.

Rozman
רוֹזְמָן
The Prešov Anchor

Moshe Aryeh Rozman invited Shimon to Prešov — the act that transplanted the Muller family to Slovakia. His daughter Sara became Shimon's wife, making Rozman the maternal root of Yakov. Robert's Hebrew name Moyshe Arie directly echoes Moshe Aryeh Rozman. Cemetery records confirm: Moshe Aryeh Rozman (d. Jan 1918), his wife Rivka bas Josef (d. Mar 1922), and Sara daughter of Moshe Aryeh wife of Simon (d. Dec 1933).

Bernfeld
בֶּרְנְפֶלְד
Topolčany — Löw-Beer connection

Moravian/Galician family confirmed among the most prominent in Topolčany. Moshe (Mor) Bernfeld (1840–1926) married Gittel Löw-Beer, directly connecting the two dynasties. His brother Jónás was born in Boskovice — same Moravian origin. The Bernfeld family built a Beth Medrash in Topolčany. Tereza-Devora Bernfeld, Robert's mother, carries this entire chain into the final generation. ~28 Bernfeld family members were murdered in the Holocaust.

Kolin
קוֹלִין
Boskovice Rabbinical Root

The Kolin identity preceded Löw-Beer. Rabbi Samuel ben Nathan Ha-Levi Kolin (b. ~1724 Kolín, d. 1806 Boskovice) headed the Boskovice yeshiva for ~60 years and authored the Machatzit HaShekel. His son Jehuda Löw married Buna Beer, creating the Löw-Beer compound name. The "Stammbaum der Familien Kolin und Löw-Beer" documents a single patrilineal line, not two merging families.

Kolín → Boskovice · 🟢 Encyclopaedia Judaica · Wikipedia

Herzfeld
הֶרְצְפֶלְד
Brezova — via Löw-Beer marriage

Meir Herzfeld (1847–1916) married Tereza Dvora Löw-Beer (daughter of Nathan Nota). Their daughter Erna Ester Herzfeld (b. 1888 Brezova, d. 1966 Haifa) married Max Bernfeld of Topolčany. Note: the Herzfeld family was from Brezova — entirely absent from the Topolčany Yizkor Book. They connected to Topolčany through the Löw-Beer marriage network, not as residents.

Shapira
שַׁפִּירָא
Rabbinic Ancestry — from Speyer

From Speyer, Germany (Latin: Spira). One of the great Ashkenazi rabbinic dynasties. The Kraków branch produced the Megale Amukot. The connection to Rabbi Samuel Kolin appears to flow through Kolin's wife ("a daughter Spira" per Rootsweb genealogy), not through his patrilineal descent. The Rashi → Shapira → Kolin claim is traditional genealogical lore, not peer-reviewed academic fact.

Robert Moyshe Arie Muller
מֹשֶׁה אַרְיֵה מִילֶר — רוּבִּי
Born: 1931  ·  Place: Prešov (Eperjes), Czechoslovakia
Father: Yakov Dov Muller  ·  Mother: Tereza-Devora Bernfeld
Paternal grandfather: Shimon Muller · Prešov liqueur factory
Maternal grandparents: Max (Mordechai) Bernfeld + Erna Herzfeld
Wife: Ruth Singer (daughter of Friedrich/Fritz Singer + Sabina/Bini Singer)
What he inherited

From his father's side: a Prešov family planted by an invitation — Moshe Aryeh Rozman calling his nephew from Sanz to work in a dry goods store. A grandfather who consulted the Munkacser Rebbe before buying a business, who commissioned a Torah scroll in grief, who traveled to Frankfurt to fight for ten days so his son could attend Yeshiva. The smell of plum liqueur and the memory of Sunday truck rides.

What he inherited

From his mother's side: the Bernfeld family of Topolčany — Gabbai, community leader, Repräsentanz deputy — connected through Gittel Löw-Beer to the great Boskovice industrial dynasty. Above them, Rabbi Samuel Kolin's 60-year yeshiva. Above him, the Shapira kabbalists of Kraków. And at the horizon of tradition: Rashi of Troyes.

His name

The Hebrew name Moyshe Arie was chosen to echo Moshe Aryeh Rozman — Shimon's uncle, the man who invited the Muller family to Prešov in the first place. Without that invitation, there would be no Muller family in Prešov; no liqueur factory; no Sunday excursions; no Robert. Through the name, the debt is remembered. The city that became their home, the factory that became their livelihood, the community that shaped them — all of it began with Moshe Aryeh's invitation. The child born at the end of that long chain carried that beginning in his name.
