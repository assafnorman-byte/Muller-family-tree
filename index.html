A Family Archive

The House of Muller
A Thousand Years to Robert

From medieval Troyes to Prešov — a Jewish family's long road
Primary narrative: Oma's Story by Denise Karasik, about her grandmother Ilona Muller · Supplemented by independent genealogical research
רוברט מִילֶר

Robert (Robi) Muller

Prešov, Slovakia — born 1931

This archive begins and ends with Robert. Every name reaches toward him; every journey leads to his door.

Born in Prešov in 1931, Robert stands at the point where a thousand years of Jewish Central European history touches living memory. Behind him are rabbis of Boskovice and Kraków, textile merchants of Moravia, community leaders of Topolčany, and — further back, by tradition — the great commentator Rashi of Troyes. Before him, the family continues.

The tabs above trace the line forward and backward. The story is long, the sources uneven, and the gaps honest. What follows is as close to the truth as we can currently stand.

```
1. Troyes, 1040

Champagne, Northern France · 11th Century

The story begins, by oral tradition, with Shlomo Yitzchaki — Rashi — born in Troyes in 1040. Merchant, vintner, and the commentator whose glosses on the Hebrew Bible and Babylonian Talmud are printed in every edition to this day. His interpretive method would shape a thousand years of Jewish learning.

Whether the House of Muller's line truly descends from Rashi is, strictly speaking, unproven. What we have is a rabbinic naming tradition — Rashi → Treves → Spira → Kolin — passed through generations as family memory, intact but academically contested. We keep it here as inheritance, not as fact.

Source: rabbinic oral tradition; Oma's Story by Denise Karasik Oral Tradition

2. The Rabbinic Chain

France → Prague → Kraków · 12th–17th Centuries

From Troyes, the line — by tradition — travels east. The Treves family produces scholars; the Spira (Shapira) family, rabbis. Nathan Neta Shapiro of Kraków (1585–1633), known as the Megaleh Amukot, is a verified historical figure and a widely accepted descendant of Rashi in rabbinic genealogies.

The link from Shapiro forward to Rabbi Samuel Kolin of Boskovice is the contested joint. Most likely it runs through Samuel's wife (a "daughter Spira" noted in older genealogies), not through his patriline. The thread is real but not where tradition placed it.

Source: rabbinic genealogies; Rootsweb Spira genealogy; cross-referenced in Denise's research Oral Tradition

3. Boskovice

Moravia, Habsburg Empire · 18th Century

Here the ground hardens. Rabbi Samuel Ha-Levi Kolin (c.1720–1806) heads the yeshiva at Boskovice for nearly sixty years, writing the Machatzit HaShekel — a commentary still printed today in standard editions of the Shulchan Aruch. His son Jehuda Löw marries Buna Beer. Their son, Salomon, takes both parental names in compliance with the 1788 Josefinian decree: Löw-Beer.

From Salomon, the line splits. One branch remains in Moravia and becomes a textile dynasty — eventually building the Villa Tugendhat in Brno. The other branch, under Salomon's son Abraham, moves east into Slovakia.

Source: Encyclopaedia Judaica; Geni; Czech Wikipedia; Villa Löw-Beer official website Verified

4. Into Slovakia

Nitra Region · Early–Mid 19th Century

Abraham Löw-Beer (c.1789–1870) crosses from Boskovice into the Nitra region of what was then Upper Hungary. His son Nathan Nota (1820–1888) is born in Topolčany — the first of the line born on Slovak soil. Around them, the town's Jewish community takes shape: Bernfelds (also from Boskovice), Herzfelds from nearby Brezova, other Löw-Beers.

The families intermarry tightly through the 19th century. This is the world into which the Topolčany nexus consolidates — the Jewish merchant-class networks that will, two generations later, produce Robert's mother's side.

Source: Geni; 1869 Jewish Hungarian Census; Topolčany Yizkor Book Verified

5. Topolčany

Slovakia · Late 19th – Early 20th Century

The Topolčany Yizkor Book records the Bernfelds and Löw-Beers as community pillars: council members, heads of the kehila, a Bernfeld Beth Medrash at the market square. Moshe Bernfeld marries Gittel Löw-Beer. Their son Max marries Erna Herzfeld — born in Brezova in 1888, daughter of Meir Herzfeld and Tereza Devora Löw-Beer.

Max and Erna have five children, among them Tereza-Devora, born December 12, 1909. She will marry Yaakov Dov Muller of Prešov, and the Topolčany nexus fuses with the Muller line.

Source: Topolčany Yizkor Book; Ancestry.com; Geni Verified

6. Prešov & Robert

Eastern Slovakia · 20th Century

On the Muller side, the Prešov Orthodox Cemetery records confirm the chain with rare precision: Moshe Aryeh Rozman, his daughter Sara, her marriage to Shimon Simon Muller. Their son Yaakov Dov Muller — named, by Ashkenazi custom, after Sara's grandfather — is the bridge.

Yaakov Dov marries Tereza-Devora Bernfeld of Topolčany. Their son is Robert (Robi) Muller, born in Prešov in 1931. His Hebrew name Moshe Arie echoes Moshe Aryeh Rozman — the great-uncle who first brought the Mullers to Prešov, whose daughter Sara became Shimon's wife. The name closes a circle. Most of the extended family is murdered in the Shoah; Erna Herzfeld Bernfeld survives and dies in Haifa in 1966. Robert lives. The story, for now, pauses here.

Source: Prešov Orthodox Cemetery records (Futtersack/Landau); Oma's Story by Denise Karasik Single-Source (Gen 3)

Shimon's Story

Nowy Sącz → Prešov → Auschwitz · ~1874–1944 · Source: Oma's Story by Denise Karasik

I. From Sanz to Prešov: The Invitation

Shimon Muller was born in Nowy Sącz — known in Jewish tradition as Sanz — the Galician town home to the great Halberstam Hasidic dynasty. He was the son of Ahron Muller, a teacher trained at the Hildesheimer Rabbinical Seminary in Berlin, and Pesel Lieberman. An invitation changed the course of his life. His uncle Moshe Aryeh Rozman called him to Prešov to work in his dry goods business — to learn the trade and earn a livelihood. Shimon came. And while working in his uncle's store, he met Sara — Moshe Aryeh's own daughter, his cousin. Sara broke a long-standing betrothal to marry him. The family was in an uproar. The marriage held. Six children followed: Ilona (Oma), Rosie (Raizel), Yakov, Margit (Malca), Pirike (Perel), and Josi.

II. The Businessman

After years working for his father-in-law, Shimon's first independent venture was a cheese factory, co-owned with partners Grosswirt and Schnitzer. Then came the opportunity to acquire Schwartz & Glassner — a fruit liqueur enterprise. Before committing, Shimon consulted the Munkacser Rebbe, one of the most revered Hasidic authorities of the era. The Rebbe advised him to buy. He did. The business flourished: plum liqueurs and raspberry syrups, stored in great multi-colored glass vats, shipped as far as Germany. The factory courtyard sat directly behind the family's spacious apartment — the smell of fruit always close.

III. A Fourteen-Year-Old Runs the Factory

During World War I, the army requisitioned Shimon's forest for timber. Russian prisoners of war were sent to log it; Shimon had to travel there to supervise, away from Prešov for long stretches. The factory could not stop. So Shimon taught his daughter Ilona — fourteen years old — how to run it entirely: balancing the ledgers, managing the workers, operating the bottling machinery. She did it. The daughter he trusted most with the work he had built.

IV. Margit's Death — A Torah Scroll for the Shul

Tragedy came through his daughter Margit (Malca), who died from internal injuries after jumping from the wrong train. Shimon commissioned a Sefer Torah to be written and donated it to the Prešov shul in her memory — a man expressing grief the only way that seemed adequate: by giving something eternal to the community she had been part of.

V. Father, Grandfather, Second Marriage

When his son Yakov was to attend yeshiva in Frankfurt, the institution was hesitant — reluctant to take a boy from a strict local yeshiva in Prešov. Shimon traveled to Frankfurt personally and advocated for ten days until they relented. After Sara's death from an infected cut, Shimon remarried Malvinka Fonfedder — Sara's cousin, who had grown up attending the same school as Sara. As a grandfather, he loved taking the grandchildren on Sunday excursions outside the city by truck or horse and wagon. Robert was born in 1931 while Shimon was very much the living center of the family.

VI. Budapest, 1944 — The Last Blessing

As German occupation advanced through Hungary in 1944, the family's options collapsed. Shimon first stayed with his daughter Rosi in Munkacs — but he spoke no local language and was too visibly a foreigner. He was smuggled to Miskolc to live with Ilona. The family made a plan to flee to Budapest with forged Aryan papers. At Budapest station, Shimon was identified as Jewish, arrested, and taken to prison. His granddaughter Gretl — herself captured and held in the same prison — spotted him across the yard during a registration lineup. She ran to him. He placed his hands on her head. He gave her his blessing. A guard chased her away. That was the last time anyone in the family saw Shimon Muller. He was deported to Auschwitz and murdered there.

Primary source: Oma's Story by Denise Karasik (Ilona Muller's account) · Yad Vashem records · Hungarian Jewish deportation records, 1944 Verified — Primary Source
```

Places ⁂

The geography of the House of Muller — a map of departures and arrivals.

```

Troyes

Champagne, France · 11th century

Rashi's home: a prosperous Champagne market town, its fairs drawing merchants from across Europe, its vineyards producing wine that traveled far. Rashi himself was a vintner as well as a scholar — a man embedded in the commercial life of his town even as he built the interpretive architecture that would define Jewish learning for a thousand years. His schoolhouse was not a great institution but a small one, in a house near the market. The commentary that emerged from it still appears in print today.

By family tradition, this is where the line begins — though the thread connecting Troyes to Boskovice runs through oral genealogy, not documents.

Kraków

Poland · 16th–17th centuries

The seat of Rabbi Nathan Neta Shapiro — the Megaleh Amukot — who served as Chief Rabbi here from 1617 until his death in 1633. In his era, Kraków was one of the great centers of Ashkenazi learning and Jewish mysticism; the city's Jewish quarter in Kazimierz was home to one of the densest concentrations of scholars in Europe. The Shapiro family's connection to this chain is oral tradition — the link forward to the Kolin line likely runs through a daughter's marriage rather than a patrilineal chain. But Rabbi Shapiro himself is a fully documented historical figure, and his world is real.

Boskovice

Moravia · 18th–19th centuries

One of the great Moravian kehillot, with a Jewish quarter dating to the 13th century. By Samuel Kolin's era the town's Jews — several hundred families — sustained a full institutional life: yeshiva, beit midrash, hospital, mikveh, and a cemetery whose oldest stones date to the 1600s. Merchants traded in cloth, grain, and livestock; the yeshiva produced rabbis who served communities across Central Europe. The Löw-Beer surname was born here in 1788, when Salomon registered under Joseph II's decree. The Bernfeld family also traced its roots to Boskovice — the same generation that produced both lineages in this archive originated in the same small Moravian town. The Jewish quarter still stands today, one of the best-preserved in Central Europe.

Featured ancestor: Rabbi Samuel Ha-Levi Kolin →

Svitávka & Brno

Moravia · 19th–20th centuries

Where the Moravian branch of the Löw-Beer family — Moses Löw-Beer (c.1794–1851), Salomon's other son — built a textile manufacturing empire. The Svitávka mill became one of the leading textile operations in 19th-century Moravia. The family's wealth eventually funded one of the 20th century's most significant works of architecture: the Villa Tugendhat in Brno, commissioned by Moses's great-granddaughter Grete Löw-Beer Tugendhat and her husband Fritz from Mies van der Rohe in 1928–1930. The villa is a UNESCO World Heritage Site. The family fled the Nazis; the villa was seized, used as a horse stable, then as a facility for Josef Mengele's research. It was restored and is open today.

Topolčany

Upper Hungary → Slovakia · 19th–20th centuries

The hinge town of this archive. Sitting at the intersection of Nitra region trade routes, Topolčany grew a Jewish community of several thousand by the early 20th century — sustained by grain merchants, textile traders, and a dense web of institutional life: a Great Synagogue, schools, a chevra kadisha, and a community Repräsentanz drawn almost entirely from its leading families. The Bernfelds, Löw-Beers, and Herzfelds appear in its leadership in successive generations. The 1869 Hungarian Jewish congress forced every community to declare as Neolog or Orthodox; Topolčany's resulting divisions shaped who married whom for decades. The Topolčany Yizkor Book (1976) documents both the community's century of prosperity and its near-total destruction in 1942, when most of the town's Jews were deported to Auschwitz.

Featured ancestor: Nathan Nota Löw-Beer →

Brezova

Western Slovakia · 19th century

A small town in the Trenčín region, with a modest Jewish community sustained primarily by trade. Erna Herzfeld was born here on July 20, 1888 — daughter of Meir Herzfeld and Tereza Devora Löw-Beer. The Herzfeld family's connection to the Topolčany network came through that maternal Löw-Beer line rather than through residence in Topolčany itself; their absence from the Topolčany Yizkor Book confirms they were Brezova people, not Topolčany people. Small enough that its Jewish community has left few other records, Brezova nevertheless produced one of this archive's most significant figures.

Prešov

Eastern Slovakia · 20th century

Robert's birthplace. In the interwar period, Prešov (then Eperjes in Hungarian) had a substantial Jewish population — both Neolog and Orthodox communities — sustained by commerce and the professions. Shimon Muller ran the Schwartz & Glassner liqueur factory here; the courtyard sat directly behind the family apartment, the smell of plum and raspberry always close. The Orthodox community maintained a cemetery whose records independently confirm the Rozman–Muller marriage chain in Hebrew stone inscriptions. Most of Prešov's Jews were deported in 1942. Robert survived.

Nowy Sącz (Sanz)

Austrian Galicia · 19th century

Shimon Muller's birthplace — known in Jewish tradition as Sanz, home of the great Halberstam Hasidic dynasty founded by Rabbi Chaim Halberstam in the 19th century. A prosperous Galician town with a large Jewish community and rich communal records. Shimon left for Prešov on his uncle Moshe Aryeh Rozman's invitation and never returned.

Haifa

Israel · 20th century

Erna Herzfeld Bernfeld's final home, and the endpoint — so far — of the family's westward-then-eastward journey across a millennium. Erna, born in Brezova in 1888, survived the destruction that took most of her Topolčany world, and died in Haifa in 1966 at 77. She was the thread that held. The city she arrived in was itself newly built — a modern Jewish city on a bay that the family's ancestors could not have imagined when they traded grain in Nagytapolcsány or wrote commentaries in Boskovice.
```

Surnames ⁂

Names are their own archive. Each one carries a place, a decree, or a forgotten grandparent.

```
Muller / מִילֶר

German origin · "miller"

An occupational surname — the miller. Extraordinarily common across German-speaking Jewry. In this family, the name traces to Prešov; the Hebrew appears as מִילֶר on the cemetery stones.

Rozman

Possibly from "Rosenmann"

The surname of Robert's paternal grandmother Sara. The Prešov cemetery preserves Moshe Aryeh Rozman's Hebrew epitaph, identifying his father as Yaakov Dov HaLevi — the grandfather for whom Robert's father Yaakov Dov was named.

Bernfeld

German · "bear field"

Among the most prominent families in Topolčany. The Yizkor Book records Moshe, Josef, and Max Bernfeld in successive leadership roles. The family also has Boskovice roots.

Löw-Beer / לוֹאו־בֵּר

Fused surname · Boskovice, 1788

Born of the Josefinian surname decree: Jehuda Löw + Buna Beer → Salomon Löw-Beer. The name carries both parental lines and becomes a marker of the Moravian–Slovak rabbinic-merchant dynasty.

Kolin

Toponymic · Kolín, Bohemia

The town of Kolín — a common surname marker for Bohemian Jewry. Rabbi Samuel Ha-Levi Kolin carried it from his birthplace to Boskovice, and the name fades in later generations as Löw-Beer takes hold.

Shapira / Spira

Toponymic · Speyer, Germany

One of the oldest continuous Ashkenazi rabbinic surnames, originating in the Rhineland city of Speyer. By rabbinic tradition, connected back to Rashi via the Treves family — though the direct link in this chain is contested.

Treves

Toponymic · Trier/Troyes

Associated in medieval Jewish scholarship with both Trier (Germany) and Troyes (France). In the family's oral genealogy, this is one of the links back to Rashi.

Luria

Rabbinic dynasty

A distinguished rabbinic surname that appears in the wider family context. Associated with Rabbi Isaac Luria of Safed (the Arizal) and his forebears — a thread running through the extended network.

Herzfeld

German · "heart field"

The surname of Erna, Robert's maternal grandmother. The family was based in Brezova, connected to the Topolčany Löw-Beers through marriage.
```

```
Featured Ancestor · Generation 17

Rabbi Samuel Ha-Levi Kolin Verified

רבי שמואל הלוי קולין
c. 1720, Kolín — 1806, Boskovice · Rosh Yeshiva, author of the Machatzit HaShekel

The world he was born into

Samuel was born around 1720 in Kolín — a prosperous Bohemian market town on the Elbe, with one of the largest Jewish communities in the Bohemian crown lands. The town's Jews lived under the Familiants Laws of 1726, which capped the number of Jewish families permitted to legally marry and settle in each locality. Only the eldest son could inherit the family's "familiants number" and remain; younger sons had to move, or remain unmarried. It was a mechanism for demographic compression that pushed ambitious younger men east — into Moravia, into Hungary, into trade routes that led further still.

Moravia itself was the intellectual heartland of Central European Jewry in this period. Towns like Boskovice, Nikolsburg (Mikulov), and Prossnitz (Prostějov) sustained large, self-governing Jewish quarters with their own schools, courts, and printing presses. The Landesrabbiner of Moravia was one of the most prestigious posts in the Ashkenazi world. Into this environment — dense, learned, and politically precarious — Samuel arrived in Boskovice and would stay for the rest of his life.

Boskovice: the town

Boskovice was one of Moravia's most significant Jewish settlements, with a Jewish quarter dating to the 13th century. By Samuel's era it contained a synagogue, a beit midrash, a mikveh, a hospital, and a cemetery whose oldest stones date to the 1600s. The quarter sat alongside the Christian town, divided by gates that were locked at night — a physical boundary that contained an entire world of commerce, scholarship, and family life. Boskovice's Jewish merchants traded in cloth, grain, and livestock; its scholars corresponded with the great yeshivot of Prague, Pressburg, and Frankfurt. Samuel presided over this world from the yeshiva, a role that made him simultaneously a religious authority, a judge, and the intellectual center of a community of several hundred families. The Jewish quarter of Boskovice still stands today, largely intact — one of the best-preserved in Central Europe.

His life's work

Samuel spent nearly sixty years at the head of the Boskovice yeshiva — an almost unbroken tenure that made him one of the most influential Moravian rabbis of the 18th century. His masterwork is the Machatzit HaShekel — a commentary on the Magen Avraham, which is itself a commentary on the Orach Chaim section of the Shulchan Aruch. A commentary on a commentary: by Samuel's generation, the tradition had layered centuries of interpretation on top of itself, and new scholarship meant engaging not just the original text but the entire accumulated conversation.

The Machatzit HaShekel was first printed in Prague in 1794. It earned a reputation for rigor and clarity — qualities valued above novelty in a world where the worst sin was misreading what came before. Within a generation it had earned permanent canonical status, and it remains printed in standard editions of the Shulchan Aruch to this day. Any yeshiva student who opens a standard Orach Chaim in 2025 encounters Samuel's words in the margins, three columns from the central text, as if the conversation never ended.

The name that became a dynasty

In 1788, Emperor Joseph II — in his Edict of Tolerance and the subsequent surname decree — required all Jews across Habsburg lands to adopt fixed, hereditary German surnames. The intent was administrative: to allow taxation, military conscription, and civil record-keeping. For families who had used Hebrew patronymics for centuries, the decree was a rupture. You could choose your name — but once chosen, it was permanent and heritable.

Samuel's son Jehuda Löw had married Buna Beer. Their son Salomon, registering under the new decree, fused both parental names: Löw-Beer. The choice was not uncommon — combining the father's and mother's surnames was one way to honor both lines in a single bureaucratic act. What Salomon could not have known was that this hyphenated invention would carry forward as a marker of the family for six more generations: through Moravian textile magnates, through Slovak community pillars, through the builders of the Villa Tugendhat, and — by a long and indirect road — to Robert in Prešov.

Why this matters to Robert

Seven generations separate Rabbi Samuel from Robert. The surname changed, the country changed, the language changed. The line held.

Featured Ancestor · Generation 21

Nathan Nota Löw-Beer Verified

נתן נטע לוֹאו־בֵּר
1820 – 1888, Topolčany · The first of the line born on Slovak soil

The world he was born into

Nathan Nota was born in 1820 in Topolčany — then Nagytapolcsány, in Nyitra county of the Kingdom of Hungary. His father Abraham had made the crossing from Boskovice into Upper Hungary some years before, drawn by conditions that were, in a narrow sense, more favorable: Jewish settlement in Hungary was regulated differently than in Moravia, and commercial opportunities in the grain and textile trades were expanding as the Hungarian economy modernized under the Habsburgs.

The Hungary into which Nathan Nota was born was still the Hungary of the Reform era — a period of intense political ferment among Magyar nobility seeking autonomy from Vienna, and simultaneously a period of growing debate within Jewish communities about modernization, language, and how much to accommodate the surrounding culture. By the time Nathan Nota died in 1888, the Hungarian Jewish community had formally split into Neolog (reformist), Orthodox, and Status Quo movements — a fracture that would shape every community's synagogue, school, and marriage politics for the next half century.

Topolčany: the town and the family's place in it

Topolčany sat at the intersection of several trade routes in the Nitra region, making it a natural center for Jewish merchant activity. The Jewish community grew from a few hundred in the early 19th century to several thousand by the early 20th, concentrated in trade, textiles, and grain. The town had a Great Synagogue, a beit midrash, Jewish schools, and an organized chevra kadisha. Governance was through the community Repräsentanz — a council whose membership overlapped almost entirely with the town's leading merchant families.

The Löw-Beers occupied this world at its upper register. The 1869 Jewish Hungarian Census records Nathan Nota's brother Maximilian as a wheat merchant — the grain trade was the family's economic spine. By Nathan Nota's later decades, the Löw-Beers, Bernfelds, and Herzfelds formed an interlocking network of families who served on the community council, funded the synagogue, educated each other's children, and — generation by generation — married each other's daughters. The Topolčany Yizkor Book records later Löw-Beers in successive leadership positions: Dr. Aladar Löw-Beer, Bernat Löw-Beer, Moses-Ahron Löw-Beer on the pre-WWI council. Nathan Nota's generation planted that root.

Noteworthy events of his era

Three upheavals shaped Nathan Nota's adult life. The 1848 Revolution convulsed the Habsburg lands: Hungarian Jews largely supported the Magyar independence movement, and many served in the revolutionary army, hoping emancipation would follow a Hungarian victory. It did not — the revolution was crushed, and full Jewish emancipation in Hungary was delayed until 1867. The Austro-Hungarian Compromise of 1867 then transformed the political landscape entirely, creating a dual monarchy in which Hungarian Jews were formally emancipated and rapidly integrated into civic and commercial life. Topolčany's Jewish community entered its most prosperous period in these decades. And the formal community split of 1869, when the Hungarian Jewish congress forced communities to declare as Neolog or Orthodox, required every family to take sides — a choice that governed which synagogue you attended, which school your children entered, and whom you could marry.

Why this matters to Robert

Without Nathan Nota's generation, the Topolčany nexus that produced Robert's maternal grandmother does not exist. Four generations separate him from Robert.

Featured Ancestor · Generation 22 · Topolčany

Moshe (Mor) Bernfeld Verified

משה בֶּרנְפֶלד
1840 – 1926, Topolčany · Patriarch of the Bernfeld dynasty, community pillar

The world he was born into

Moshe was born in 1840 in Topolčany — but his roots, like the Löw-Beers', were in Boskovice. His brother Jónás (Yonah Tzvi) was born in Boskovice in 1831, which means the Bernfeld family made the same Moravia–Slovakia crossing as the Löw-Beers, in roughly the same generation, from the same town. Two families with the same origin, arriving in the same destination: the intermarriages that followed were not coincidental.

Moshe lived to 86 — an almost impossible span for the era — which means he was born under Austrian rule, survived the 1848 revolution, saw Hungarian Jewish emancipation in 1867, weathered the Orthodox/Neolog split of 1869, lived through World War I, the collapse of the Habsburg Empire, the birth of Czechoslovakia, and died in 1926 just as the fragile interwar order was consolidating. He outlasted empires.

His family business and the town

The Bernfelds were merchant-class: grain, textiles, and the trade networks that moved goods between Slovak towns and the larger Austro-Hungarian economy. The family's prominence in Topolčany was institutional as well as commercial. The Topolčany Yizkor Book records a Bernfeld Beth Medrash built at the market square — a study house funded and attached to the family, a common marker of leadership in Central European Jewish towns. Moshe himself served as deputy chairman of the community Repräsentanz in the pre-WWI period, alongside figures like Josef Bernfeld and David Löw-Beer as successive heads of the community.

The marriage that joined the dynasties

Moshe married Gittel (Katalin) née Löw-Beer — a daughter of the same Löw-Beer line that had arrived from Boskovice a generation earlier. The marriage was the dynastic fusion: Bernfeld merchant prominence meeting Löw-Beer rabbinic-commercial lineage. Their son Max (Mordechai), born 1884, would carry the combined weight of both families into the 20th century — and marry Erna Herzfeld, binding in yet another Boskovice-descended line.

Featured Ancestor · Generation 23 · Brezova → Topolčany → Haifa

Erna Ester Herzfeld Bernfeld Verified

אסתר הרצפלד־בֶּרנְפֶלד
July 20, 1888 Brezova — February 1, 1966 Haifa · The survivor. The thread that held.

The world she was born into

Erna was born on July 20, 1888 in Brezova — a small town in the Trenčín region of western Slovakia, then part of the Kingdom of Hungary. Her father Meir Herzfeld had married Tereza Devora Löw-Beer, a daughter of Nathan Nota Löw-Beer of Topolčany, which connected the Brezova Herzfelds to the larger Topolčany merchant-community network without making them Topolčany residents. Erna grew up in a small-town Jewish community, the daughter of a family connected upward by marriage to one of the region's most prominent families.

By the time she was an adult, the world she was born into — the Austro-Hungarian Jewish world of small-town commerce and community institutions — was already under pressure. World War I (1914–1918) collapsed the empire, created Czechoslovakia, and left Slovak Jewish communities navigating a new nation-state whose relationship to its Jewish minority was uncertain and often hostile.

Life in Topolčany: prosperity and its limits

Erna married Max (Mordechai) Bernfeld of Topolčany, the son of the community's most prominent family. Together they had five children. Max served as Gabbai of the Great Synagogue and a council member of the Repräsentanz. The family occupied the upper tier of Topolčany Jewish life through the 1920s and 1930s — precisely the period in which Slovak antisemitism was intensifying, the Slovak People's Party was consolidating power, and the conditions for what would follow were being built, incrementally, in plain sight.

The Topolčany Yizkor Book records approximately 28 Bernfeld family members who perished in the Shoah. Max's brother Sámuel Yehuda Bernfeld died at Auschwitz around 1944. The community that had produced generations of leadership, commerce, and institutional life was nearly erased in 1942.

Survival and Haifa

How exactly Erna and Max survived is not documented in the records accessible to this archive. What we know is the outcome: Max died in 1964, Erna in Haifa on February 1, 1966 at 77. They made it out. She crossed from the world of Austro-Hungarian Jewish provincial life to the State of Israel — a journey that covered not just geography but the entirety of the 20th century's catastrophic transformation of European Jewry. That she died in Haifa, at peace, in a country that did not yet exist when she was born, is one of the archive's quietest and most significant facts.

Why this matters to Robert

Erna is Robert's maternal grandmother. She is also, within this archive, the figure whose survival most concretely confirms the line forward. Without her reaching Haifa, the chain breaks.

Moshe Aryeh (Ariel) Rozman Verified

משה אריה רוֹזְמָן
d. 5 Shevat 5678 (January 1918), Prešov · Confirmed by cemetery stone

What we know, and how we know it

Moshe Aryeh Rozman is one of the few figures in this archive confirmed not by a database or a family tree, but by a physical object that survived him. The Prešov Orthodox Cemetery records, compiled from Hebrew inscriptions by Michael R. Futtersack from ledgers provided by Desidor Landau of the Prešov Jewish Community, record: ROZMAN — Mose Ariel ben Jakov Dov Halevi, buried 5 Shevat 5678 (January 1918).

"Mose Ariel" is Moshe Aryeh — the names אריאל and אריה are near-interchangeable in Hebrew epitaphs. "Ben Yaakov Dov HaLevi" identifies his father. This stone independently establishes that Moshe Aryeh Rozman existed in Prešov, died in January 1918, and was the son of one Yaakov Dov HaLevi — the grandfather after whom Robert's father would be named, according to Ashkenazi custom.

Prešov in his era

Moshe Aryeh lived through the peak era of Prešov Jewish life. In the decades before WWI, Prešov (then Eperjes in Hungarian) was a prosperous provincial city in Upper Hungary, home to both a Neolog and an Orthodox Jewish community — the Orthodox community maintaining its own synagogue, school, and the cemetery whose stones record his name. He died in January 1918, two months before the end of the First World War, in a city that was about to change countries: Prešov would become part of the new state of Czechoslovakia in November 1918.

His family, confirmed in stone

The same Prešov cemetery records also confirm his wife: ROZMAN — Rivre bas Josef, Zena Mose Ariel, buried 22 Adar 5682 (March 1922). "Rivre" is Rivka; "Zena Mose Ariel" means wife of Moshe Aryeh. Her patronymic "bas Josef" connects to a Josef Lieberman also buried in the same cemetery (November 1916), supporting the family memory that Rivka's maiden name was Lieberman. A third stone confirms the next generation: MILLER — Sure bas Mose Arie Halevi, Zena Simon — Sara, daughter of Moshe Aryeh, wife of Simon (Shimon Muller). The family chain, inscribed in Hebrew on stones in a Prešov cemetery, is its own verification document.

Why this matters to Robert

Moshe Aryeh is Robert's paternal great-grandfather. His stone is the physical-world anchor of the Muller side of the archive.

Rabbi Nathan Neta Shapiro Oral Tradition

רבי נתן נטע שַׁפִּירָא — מְגַלֵּה עֲמֻקּוֹת
1585 – 1633, Kraków · Chief Rabbi of Kraków, Kabbalist, author of Megaleh Amukot

The world he was born into

Nathan Neta Shapiro was born in 1585 in Kraków — at the time one of the most significant centers of Jewish learning in the world. The Jewish quarter of Kazimierz, adjacent to the royal capital, was home to thousands of Jews, a dozen synagogues, and a density of scholarship that rivaled any community in Europe. The Shapiro (Spira) family were already a distinguished rabbinic dynasty, tracing their own name back to Speyer on the Rhine — one of the great medieval Jewish communities destroyed in the Crusades, whose survivors scattered east into Poland and became the Ashkenazi establishment.

He came of age during the period of Polish Jewish intellectual flowering that produced the Maharal of Prague, the Rama (Rabbi Moses Isserles), and their immediate successors — a generation that set the normative framework of Ashkenazi Jewish law and mysticism for centuries.

His life's work: the Megaleh Amukot

Nathan Neta served as Chief Rabbi of Kraków from 1617 until his death in 1633 — one of the most prestigious rabbinic posts in the Ashkenazi world. His major work, the Megaleh Amukot ("Revealer of Deep Things"), is a Kabbalistic commentary on the Torah that became a foundational text of Polish Jewish mysticism. The title is a reference to Daniel 2:22 — "He reveals deep and hidden things." The work is dense, multilayered, and numerological, characteristic of the Lurianic Kabbalah that had spread from Safed into Polish Jewish intellectual culture in the late 16th century.

He died in 1633, leaving the Megaleh Amukot as his lasting contribution to the tradition — a work still studied in mystical circles today.

The contested link to this family

The Shapira/Spira → Rashi descent is well-established in rabbinic genealogical tradition and widely accepted. The connection forward from the Shapiro line to Rabbi Samuel Kolin of Boskovice is where the chain becomes contested. The most likely path, based on available genealogical research, runs through Samuel Kolin's wife — identified in older genealogies as "a daughter Spira" — rather than through his patriline. Samuel's father bore the given name "Nathan Neta HaLevi," which shares the given name with the Kraków Chief Rabbi, possibly contributing to conflation in family memory.

Why this matters to Robert — with caveat

If the oral tradition holds, Nathan Neta Shapiro sits approximately eleven generations before Robert, and provides the link from the Rashi line into the documented Boskovice chain. We hold this as family inheritance — the tradition that for generations has placed this family in the company of Kraków's greatest rabbi — while being honest that the academic chain of evidence has not closed this link.

This connection is oral tradition, not academically verified. Nathan Neta Shapiro is a fully documented historical figure. His place in this family's chain is not. We include him because the tradition is real and worth preserving — and because his world illuminates where the rabbinic line came from.

Grete Löw-Beer Tugendhat Verified

גרטה לוֹאו־בֵּר טוּגֶנְדְהָט
1903 – 1970 · Commissioned the Villa Tugendhat · Fled the Nazis · UNESCO World Heritage

Who she was

Grete Löw-Beer was born in 1903 in Brno, the great-granddaughter of Moses Löw-Beer (c.1794–1851) — Salomon Löw-Beer's son who stayed in Moravia when his brother Abraham moved to Slovakia. Moses built a textile manufacturing dynasty in Svitávka; over three generations the family became prosperous, culturally assimilated, and architecturally ambitious. Grete's father Alfred Löw-Beer was a successful industrialist. She was educated, cosmopolitan, and at home in the world of Weimar-era European modernism.

In 1928, she married Fritz Tugendhat, a businessman from a Jewish textile family. That same year, they commissioned a house.

The Villa

The architect was Ludwig Mies van der Rohe, then at the peak of his European career. The Villa Tugendhat, completed in 1930, became one of the defining works of 20th-century modernism: open plan, floor-to-ceiling glass walls facing the garden, onyx and macassar ebony panels, a chromium cross-shaped column that has been reproduced in architecture schools ever since. It expressed — in steel and glass — a vision of Jewish bourgeois modernity that was about to be destroyed.

Grete and Fritz fled to Switzerland in 1938, weeks before Kristallnacht. The villa was seized by the Nazis, used as a horse stable, then as a facility for Josef Mengele's research on twin children from the camps. After the war it became a children's rehabilitation clinic. It was restored by the Czech state and opened as a museum. In 2001 it was designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site — one of only a handful of 20th-century buildings to receive that designation.

After the villa: Venezuela and the end

Grete and Fritz eventually made it to Venezuela, where they built a new life. They divorced in the 1940s. Grete died in 1970, having lived long enough to see the villa she had commissioned become a monument — but not long enough to see its full restoration. Her story is the collateral Löw-Beer story: what the family name could become when it had the resources and the era to express itself fully, and what that world was worth to the forces that erased it.

Her connection to Robert

Grete is not in Robert's direct line. She descends from Moses Löw-Beer (the Moravian branch); Robert descends from Abraham Löw-Beer (the Slovak branch) — brothers, sons of Salomon. Their most recent common ancestor is Salomon Löw-Beer of Boskovice (1776–1832). They are collateral — a shared root, two very different trajectories.

Collateral branch. Grete's story is included here because it illuminates the full scope of what the Löw-Beer name represented — and because the Villa Tugendhat is a physical monument to the same family tree that leads to Robert, even if by a different branch.
```
